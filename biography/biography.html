Behind the Screen A Journey Built by Code and Circuits

Childhood Years

My childhood was deeply influenced by the world of computers and technology. My father owned a small computer shop, which became my second home. While other children played outside, I spent my time watching computer parts being assembled. I was fascinated by the green motherboards and tiny electronic components. Every day felt like a new lesson inside that shop. I learned early that computers were not just machines but systems that required harmony. My father taught me patience and discipline when handling hardware. I still remember holding a processor carefully, afraid of bending its pins. This moment sparked my curiosity about how technology worked. I began asking questions about RAM, hard drives, and power supplies. Watching was no longer enough for me. I wanted to participate and build something myself. My father guided me step by step as I installed components. The sound of a component clicking into place excited me. Each successful build gave me confidence. I learned the importance of compatibility and organization. Cable management taught me discipline and cleanliness. Technology stopped being mysterious and became familiar. I felt proud when friends asked for help fixing their computers. Those childhood experiences shaped my love for technology.

Teenage Years

As I entered my teenage years, my hobby evolved into a serious skill. In high school, my teachers noticed my strong hardware knowledge. I was chosen to represent my school in a PC assembly competition. The pressure was intense, but I trusted my experience. I practiced assembling computers repeatedly at home. Speed and accuracy became my focus. On competition day, I relied on muscle memory. My hands moved confidently, just like my father taught me. I placed second place, which filled me with pride. That achievement motivated me to aim higher. In senior high school, I joined a robotics project using Arduino. I worked on motors, sensors, and wiring. I realized hardware needed software to function properly. This realization pushed me to learn programming. I enjoyed writing logic that controlled machines. Our robot project won Best Thesis Group Project. This proved my ability in both hardware and coding. I competed against other skilled students from different schools. These experiences broadened my perspective on technology. My teenage years connected my past to my future.

College Years

Entering college forced me to make an important decision. Many expected me to take BSIT because of my hardware skills. Instead, I chose Bachelor of Science in Computer Science. I wanted to understand how computers think and process data. Building hardware alone was no longer enough for me. Computer science challenged my problem-solving abilities. I learned programming languages and algorithms. Abstract thinking became part of my daily routine. My hardware background gave me an advantage. I understood how software interacts with physical machines. This dual skill set strengthened my confidence. I continued to fix computers while learning to code. Debugging programs felt similar to troubleshooting hardware. I realized my past experiences still mattered. I consider hardware knowledge a valuable asset. My goal is to work in a leading tech company. I also dream of building my own technology business. College helped me discover my true potential. I now see technology as both art and science. This journey excites me as I move toward the future.
